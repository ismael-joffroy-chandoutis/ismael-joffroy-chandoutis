## 1. Why this word, why now

"Techno-fascism" became, in 2024-2026, a catch-all word thrown around everywhere. My goal here is **not** to wield it as a slogan, but to **put it through the sieve**: where does the concept come from, what does it rigorously cover, **and does it hold up analytically** against serious definitions of fascism? The conclusion will be **nuanced** — neither trivialization nor verbal inflation.

The contemporary trigger is plain: at Trump's inauguration (January 2025), the bosses of Apple, Amazon, Google and Meta paid to sit in the front row, **Musk made a gesture interpreted as a fascist salute** (Part 9), and a tech-right coalition installed itself at the heart of the state (Parts 2, 8). Hence the question.

## 2. Genealogy of the concept

The term has **three distinct roots**, not to be confused.

### a) Historiographical techno-fascism (Janis Mimura)
Historian **Janis Mimura** coined "techno-fascism" to analyze the **"reform bureaucrats"** of **imperial Japan and Manchukuo** (1930s-40s). She defines it as a **"radical, authoritarian form of technocracy"** fusing the **totalizing state** with the agencies of **military and bureaucratic planning**. → The hard core: **technocracy + authoritarianism**, expertise placed in the service of a total state.

### b) "Reactionary modernism" (Jeffrey Herf)
A major concept (1984) for understanding the fascism/technology link. Herf describes the **paradox** of the German Conservative Revolution and of Nazism: an **"immense enthusiasm for modern technology" coupled with a "rejection of the Enlightenment and of the values and institutions of liberal democracy."** A **"highly technological romanticism,"** embodied by **Ernst Jünger, Oswald Spengler, Carl Schmitt, Hans Freyer**.

> 🔑 This is **the** key to the dossier: fascism is **not** anti-technology. It can **worship the machine while hating democracy and critical reason**. Exactly the structure found in the tech-right: *maximum technological acceleration + contempt for liberal democracy.* (Cf. **Thiel**: "freedom and democracy are no longer compatible," Part 2.)

### c) Futurism (Marinetti)
The aesthetic matrix: exaltation of speed, the machine, force, contempt for the past — and the slide toward the **Fascist Manifesto** of 1919. Andreessen paraphrases Marinetti in his *Techno-Optimist Manifesto*. → Covered in **[Part 6](./marinetti-futurisme-fascisme.md)**.

## 3. The contemporary definition (Coeckelbergh)

Philosopher **Mark Coeckelbergh** (University of Vienna) provides the most developed contemporary formulation. For him, techno-fascism has **three components**:
1. **The technology** itself: AI and platforms;
2. **The economic context**: Big Tech + **surveillance capitalism** (Zuboff);
3. **The political context**: right-wing populism, authoritarianism, illiberal democracy.

**The displacement of mechanisms — the most important idea:**

| Historical fascism (1930s-40s) | Techno-fascism (2020s) |
|---|---|
| Mass rallies, open terror | **Algorithms** that predict, *nudge*, constrain — **often invisible** |
| Dominates through **fear** | **Pacifies through pleasure**, convenience, distraction |
| Mass propaganda | **Informational domination**, targeted emotional manipulation |
| Political police | **Surveillance** + automation of bureaucratic decisions |

Coeckelbergh speaks of a slide toward **"digital authoritarianism,"** even **"digital totalitarianism"**: AI erodes **autonomy, moral judgment and accountability**. In April 2026, he analyzed the manifesto of **Alex Karp / Palantir** (*The Technological Republic*) as **"technofascism in plain sight"** — cf. Part 9 on Karp.

> The crucial difference from 1933: **techno-fascism needs neither boots nor brown shirts.** It works through **infrastructure, addiction and algorithmic opacity**. It is a fascism **soft in form, hard in structure**.

## 4. What is fascism? The test grids

To judge the analogy, a reference is needed. Three grids carry authority.

**Robert Paxton** (the canonical definition): *"a form of political behavior marked by obsessive preoccupation with community decline, humiliation, or victimhood and by compensatory cults of unity, energy, and purity, in which a mass-based party of committed nationalist militants… abandons democratic liberties and pursues with redemptive violence and without ethical or legal restraints goals of internal cleansing and external expansion."*

**Umberto Eco — *Ur-Fascism* (1995)**: fascism is a **"fuzzy totalitarianism, a collage of different ideas, a beehive of contradictions."** He lists **14 traits** (cult of tradition + rejection of the Enlightenment; fear of difference; appeal to a frustrated middle class; obsession with a plot; contempt for the weak; "newspeak"…). Eco specifies: **it is enough that one of them be present** for fascism to "coagulate" around it.

**Jason Stanley — *How Fascism Works***: emphasizes the mechanisms — **mythic past, propaganda, anti-intellectualism, hierarchy, victimhood, law & order, sexual anxiety, the sense of "us" vs "them."**

---

## 5. The test: traits present / absent

Let's confront the tech-right of 2026 with the grids above — **honestly, in both directions.**

**✅ Fascist traits present:**
- **Contempt for the Enlightenment / liberal democracy**: Thiel ("freedom ≠ democracy"), Yarvin (CEO-monarchy, "RAGE"), pure reactionary modernism.
- **Cult of the great man / the founder**: the CEO-king (Yarvin, Karp), the heroic creator (Rand in the *Manifesto*).
- **Internal purification**: dismantling of DEI, rhetoric of the **"dilution"** of the people (Musk to the AfD), purge of the civil service (RAGE).
- **Myth of decline and rebirth**: "the West is declining, technology will save us" (Karp, *American Dynamism*).
- **Redemptive violence (symbolic)**: Musk's gesture; the rhetoric of the enemy within (Vance: "the threat comes from within").
- **Collaboration of traditional elites + insurgents**: exactly Paxton's "uneasy but effective collaboration" (tech oligarchs + MAGA).

**❌ Traits absent or divergent:**
- **No classic expansionist nationalism**: the tech-right aims rather at **"exit"** (secession, charter cities, Mars, bunkers) than territorial conquest. It is even **the inverse**: leaving the nation, not enlarging it.
- **No mass party**: this is an **oligarchy**, not a movement of millions of uniformed militants.
- **No mass mobilization**: on the contrary, **pacification and depoliticization** through distraction (Coeckelbergh).

## 6. The debate: is it *really* fascism?

The honest point: **the specialists are divided.**

**For (or "fascist tendencies"):**
- **Jason Stanley** (March 2025): the US is tipping into a fascist dictatorship — he leaves the country for Canada.
- **Robert Paxton**: long reluctant, he **changed his mind after January 6, 2021** (the coup resembles the March on Rome).
- **Coeckelbergh, Klein**: yes, in a **new** (technological) form.

**Against (or "something else"):**
- **Richard Evans** (Cambridge): Trump is **not** a fascist — no actual coup, **no thirst for conquering expansion**.
- **Al Jazeera / some analysts**: this is **"hypercapitalism,"** not fascism — "just as dangerous," but distinct.
- **Cox & O'Connor**: the **economic policies** (neoliberal) are too different from historical fascism.
- **The middle position**: **"proto-fascism"** / intensified **"fascistic tendencies"** rooted in neoliberalism — not interwar fascism.

**The most original contribution — Naomi Klein, "End Times Fascism":**
> The fascism of the 1930s-40s had **a horizon**: a utopian future (millenarian, the "new man"). The tech-right, by contrast, is **"actively betting against the future"**: Mars as a **lifeboat** for the few, **bunkers** in New Zealand to **survive** the collapse rather than reform it. It is a **fascism without a collective future** — apocalyptic, secessionist, "end times." A **major difference** from the classic model.

## 7. Untangling the neighboring vocabulary

"Techno-fascism" is only one label among several competitors. Distinguishing them avoids confusion:

| Concept | Author(s) | Central idea | Relation to fascism |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Techno-fascism** | Coeckelbergh, Mimura | Authoritarian technocracy + AI/surveillance + populism | Asserts the continuity |
| **Reactionary modernism** | Herf | Tech + anti-Enlightenment/democracy | Historical structure of fascism |
| **Surveillance capitalism** | Zuboff | Behavioral data as power | A mechanism, not a regime |
| **Technofeudalism** | Varoufakis | Platforms = feudal rent | *Post*-capitalism, not fascism |
| **Crack-Up Capitalism** | Slobodian | "Exit": capitalism **without** democracy (private zones) | Secession, not conquest |
| **"Nerd Reich"** | Gil Duran | Silicon Valley's ruling caste undermines democracy | Polemical/journalistic |
| **Techno-libertarianism** | (critique) | Radical freedom through tech | Often **superseded**: fusion with the state |

**The most decisive point:** this is **no longer libertarianism**. Critics (Truthout, The Unpopulist) show that the tech-right does **not** want "less state": it wants to **fuse with the state** ("state symbiosis" — Palantir, DOGE, Vance as VP). And **public-private fusion** is precisely, in classical theory, **a marker of fascism** (the inverse of communism, which nationalizes property: fascism **incorporates private property into the state apparatus**). This is the strongest argument in favor of the word.

## 8. Working definition

At the end of the inquiry, here is the **operational definition** I retain for the series:

> **Techno-fascism (working def., 2026):** an emerging — still partial — regime that combines
> 1. an **authoritarian technocracy** (tech expertise legitimizes power against the vote);
> 2. a **fusion of the tech oligarchy ↔ state apparatus** (Palantir, DOGE, the VP);
> 3. an **avowed contempt for liberal democracy and the Enlightenment** (Thiel, Yarvin), in the lineage of **reactionary modernism**;
> 4. **algorithmic mechanisms of control** (surveillance, *nudging*, pacification through distraction) replacing mass terror;
> 5. a logic **not of expansion but of "exit"** (secession, bunkers, Mars) — hence its **"end times"** coloring (Klein).

**Nuanced verdict:**
- Speaking of **"fascism"** in the strict 1933 sense is **inaccurate**: no mass party, no expansionism, no mobilization — rather the opposite (exit, pacification).
- But speaking of **reactionary modernism + authoritarian technocracy + state/oligarchy fusion** is **rigorously defensible** — and these ingredients **share the structural DNA** of fascism (contempt for democracy, cult of the leader, purification, myth of decline/rebirth).
- The term **"techno-fascism"** is therefore **justified as a structural analogy and a warning**, provided its **differences** are specified (secessionist rather than conquering; soft in form, hard in structure). Neither trivialization nor crying wolf: a **fascism of a new type, whose very novelty is the trap**.

> In one sentence: **techno-fascism is not the return of the brown shirts — it is democracy hollowed out from within by infrastructure, while the elite prepares its exit.**
