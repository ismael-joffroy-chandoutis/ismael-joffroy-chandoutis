# The Dossier's Argument (Synthesis)

> Synthesis text. Written June 5, 2026. This is the dossier's entry point: what it argues, after 31 parts. To be read first, or last.
> It replaces the function of Part 5 (cartography/foresight), written when the dossier had only ten parts and before the material and Chinese turn.

---

## In One Sentence

The danger is not that artificial intelligence becomes too powerful. The danger is that its current development requires, economically, energetically, and politically, that no limit be opposable to it. "Techno-fascism" is the mobilizing name for this demand for the limitless. This dossier tries to make it a precise diagnosis rather than a slogan.

## The Wrong Axis and the Right Ones

The first thing the dossier had to abandon is the left/right axis. It does not carve up this milieu. Yann LeCun is anti-doom, pro-open-source, and anti-Trump (Part 18). Geoffrey Hinton answers "socialism" when asked for the solution. Peter Thiel funds both Palantir and the vice-presidency. The word "fascism" itself, applied to people who call themselves libertarians or accelerationists, does not hold if it is reduced to a partisan label.

Two axes, by contrast, organize everything:

- **Axis 1, speed: accelerate or slow down.** This is the most visible and most talkative axis. Andreessen and e/acc push at full throttle; Yudkowsky and the doomers want to stop everything (Parts 1, 11, 14).
- **Axis 2, power: concentrate or democratize.** This is the axis that really matters. Who decides the fate of the tool, and on what basis: an oligarchy of the knowing, or accountable and revocable institutions (Part 12).

The dossier's central thesis lies in the relationship between these two axes: **they are orthogonal, and the public debate concentrates on the first so as not to have to deal with the second.**

## The Doom-vs-e/acc Debate Is a Diversion

This is the most solid conclusion of the series. The two camps that seem to clash, the accelerationists and the apocalyptics, share the same metaphysics: AGI is coming, it is total, it is near, and it is so enormous that ordinary politics no longer applies. This metaphysics serves everyone. It inflates valuations, it justifies regimes of exception, and it crushes the boring but real questions: the water of the datacenters, the Kenyan wages at $1.32 an hour, the NOx of Memphis (Part 16).

Dario Amodei is the pure case (Parts 18, 20): he announces 20% unemployment and perhaps the end of the world, and he files a $965-billion IPO the same week. This is not hypocrisy, it is worse: it is sincere, and the system produces the race anyway. While the two camps argue over axis 1, they are on the same side of axis 2: concentration.

## The Material Turn: It Is Not Just an Ideology

The second half of the dossier (Parts 15 to 26) brought the analysis down from ideas to matter, and that is where it becomes irrefutable.

- **Power has three layers**: ideas, capital, and matter (compute, energy, minerals, labor of the South). The third is the most concentrated of all: one company for the GPUs (NVIDIA), one for the etching (TSMC), one for the etching machines (ASML), and it is the least democratically governed (Parts 16, 23).
- **Extraction is not collateral damage, it is the model**: the AI value chain reproduces colonial geography, extraction in the South, value in the North (Karen Hao, Part 16).
- **And there is now an operational apparatus**: ICE's ImmigrationOS, the confidence score on a person's address, Flock's 40,000 cameras, Anduril's autonomous towers with their legislative monopoly (Part 26). It is the most material instantiation of the thesis. Fascism stops being a metaphor when you look at the contract, the sensor, and the score.

## On the Word "Fascism"

The dossier uses it, but knowing what it illuminates and what it misses. It misses precision: there are no uniformed masses, no single party, no total mobilization. More exact names exist and the dossier has explored them: Chatonsky's **vectofascism** (a power that modulates without showing itself, Part 24), Varoufakis's **technofeudalism** (the cloud fiefs from which one can no longer exit, Part 10), the plain **oligarchic capture of the state** (the Thielverse, Parts 2, 9).

But the word holds if it is founded on a genealogy rather than on an atmospheric analogy. That genealogy, the dossier built piece by piece: Marinetti and futurism (Part 6), Nick Land and dark accelerationism (Part 7), Dugin and Traditionalism via Bannon (Part 27), the eugenic substrate of TESCREAL (Part 12 and the Gebru/Torres critique), virilism and pronatalism (Parts 28, 29). When these sources are aligned, the word "fascism" stops being an insult and becomes a documented lineage. That is the difference between a tweet and a dossier.

And the most accurate rhyme is functional, not ideological: a private apparatus built to identify, track, and remove a targeted population, with aesthetic denial built in ("we track objects, not individuals"; "a surveillance database? no"). What was missing from 20th-century fascism, instant filing and the total fusion of databases, is here, and it is outsourced to publicly traded companies.

## There Is No Clean Outside

The dossier does not claim to look down on its object from above. The critics it cites are themselves embedded: Crawford wrote her critique of extraction from Microsoft's payroll, Gebru was fired from Google, the foundations that fund critical research come from tech fortunes (Part 16). The absence of an outside is itself a finding: it is technofeudalism, one no longer exits the fief.

And the limit case of this absence of an outside is this very dossier, written by an AI produced by Anthropic, one of the actors it documents (see the [authorship note](./note-autorat-ia.md)). This is not disqualifying, but it must be named. The value of this work is not in an impossible objectivity, it is in the sourcing and in the structure.

## The Wager, and What Can Bend the Trajectory

If the danger is the demand for the limitless, then every solution is a way of making a limit opposable (Part 25). Physics already imposes some (grid queues, water, turbines out of stock), it is the only regulator that lobbying cannot buy. Labor law has already seized the algorithm (the European platform directive). The terrain already blocks concrete (25 datacenters canceled in Virginia). American federalism has already held (the federal moratorium defeated 99 to 1). None of this stops the machine, but all of it slows it down, documents it, and makes it contestable.

The conclusion is therefore neither doomer nor optimist. It is political: the earth does not sink with AI, it sinks with the limitless. And the limitless, for its part, has addresses, balance sheets, and building permits. That is to say, handles.

## Reception: A Convergent Diagnosis, Outside the Tech Field

A sign that axis 2 is not a figment of the mind: it is now stated, almost identically, outside tech. In May 2026, Leo XIV's encyclical *Magnifica humanitas* devotes most of its argument to AI. As read by the philosopher Anne Alombert (*De la bêtise artificielle*, Allia, 2025) in *Le Monde*, it shifts the same lines as this dossier:

- it refuses the "for or against AI" framing and the idea that it would suffice to "align" models with our values: AI is a political question (who develops it, under which economic models, for which projects), not merely a moral one. This is our move from axis 1 to axis 2;
- it opposes two technopolitical projects, Babel (uniformization, the omnipotence of a few) and Jerusalem (diversity of skills, contributions of the many): in biblical typology, this is our concentrate↔democratize axis;
- it makes public action and the common good the source of limits opposable to the platforms: this is our thesis of the limitless (Part 25, "making a limit opposable");
- it roots the problem in matter (click workers, resource exhaustion) and in algorithmic architecture rather than in the mere "hunt for false information": this is our material turn (Part 16) and our shift from the moral to the structural;
- and it regrets the absence of named alternatives (Wikipedia, Tournesol, Pol.is): this is our "democratize" pole (Part 30).

Two gaps, which must be named so as not to make the text say more than it does. First, the encyclical stops at the diagnosis: it does not utter the word "fascism" and does not build the genealogy (Marinetti, Land, Dugin, eugenics) that, here, turns analogy into lineage. Second, it speaks from a relative exteriority that this dossier declared nonexistent ("there is no clean outside"): the Church is not funded by tech fortunes. This convergence is reached by another intellectual route, the Stiegler lineage (pharmacology of technology, the contributory economy, which Alombert cites with Tournesol and Pol.is), where this dossier goes through political economy and the genealogy of ideas. Two foreign apparatuses landing on the same hidden axis: that is what strengthens the diagnosis, rather than merely repeating it.
