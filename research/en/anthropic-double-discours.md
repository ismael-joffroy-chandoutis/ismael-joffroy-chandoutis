## 1. The Week of June 1-5, 2026: Two Discourses in 96 Hours

The sequence, day by day:

| Date | Event |
|---|---|
| **May 28** | Series H: **$65B raised, post-money valuation ~$965B**, Anthropic surpasses OpenAI ($852B) in private value. Claude Opus 4.8 released the same day. |
| **June 1** | **Confidential S-1 filing with the SEC** (IPO targeted as early as October 2026, law firm Wilson Sonsini, the one that handled Google's 2004 IPO). Run-rate ~$47B (vs. ~$9B at the end of 2025). |
| **June 2** | Extension of **Project Glasswing** (distribution of the Claude Mythos cyber model) to ~150 new organizations in 15+ countries. |
| **June 4** | The Anthropic Institute publishes **"When AI Builds Itself"** (Marina Favaro & Jack Clark): a call for the world to have "the option" to slow or temporarily pause frontier AI development. |
| **June 5** | Press wave (CNN, Fortune, CNBC, SiliconANGLE) highlighting the timing contradiction. Jack Clark to Anderson Cooper: "When I look down at the car we're driving, all I have is a gas pedal. I don't have a brake pedal." |

**What the June 4 appeal actually says**, the popular framing "Anthropic wants to pause everything" is inaccurate. The exact wording: "We believe it would be good for the world to have the **option** to slow or temporarily pause frontier AI development." And the condition: "If such systems existed, we expect that we would slow down or temporarily pause, **if other developers at or near the frontier also did so in a verifiable manner**" (multiple labs, multiple countries, USA and China included, verification). It is a call for an *optional, conditional, collective, and future* pause, not a decision, not a unilateral commitment, and not a pause of their own products.

**The text's central argument**: "recursive self-improvement" (an AI "capable of fully autonomously designing and developing its own successor"). Internal data put forward in support: **in May 2026, more than 80% of the code merged into Anthropic's codebase was written by Claude** (versus single digits before Claude Code, February 2025). "The evidence suggests that the human role is narrowing at each step in the AI development process." Jack Clark (BBC Newsnight): 100% AI-written code is "possible within two years."

**The text's honesty, to its credit**: Anthropic relativizes its own figures ("We are not there yet, and recursive self-improvement is not inevitable"; the "8x" productivity figure is "almost certainly an overstatement"). The text that warns about acceleration is also the one *selling* it, that is the most solid and sourceable internal contradiction: a single document that simultaneously makes the commercial demonstration (80% of the code) and the existential warning.

**The reactions**: Holger Mueller (Constellation Research): "Is it trying to freeze the status quo so it can catch up, or simply retain its lead? A freeze would certainly help Anthropic to maintain its leading position in B2B AI systems." Rob Enderle: the post is "more about strategic marketing than any concrete initiative to actually rein in AI developers." Fortune: "The timing is raising some eyebrows. Just last week, the AI company filed confidential paperwork to prepare for an IPO."

> ⚠️ **Documentary trap identified**: the EA Forum post "Anthropic's Pause is the Most Expensive Alarm in Corporate History" is an **April Fools' satirical fiction** (tagged as such, written with the help of "Claude Opus 4.6"). Everything in it is invented: the actual training pause, the secondary trading freeze, the "suspended" IPO, the "$800B evaporated." Several automated summaries mix this fiction with real facts. Anthropic has **not** paused its training.

## 2. Claude Mythos: Real, Not a Rumor

- **Claude Mythos exists**: a frontier model specialized in cybersecurity, revealed by an accidental CMS leak on March 27, 2026, officially announced on **April 7, 2026** (red.anthropic.com). Claimed capability: identification of thousands of zero-day vulnerabilities "in every major operating system and every major web browser" (a claim since raised to "10,000+ high/critical", self-reported figures; the only independent count: Cloudflare, ~2,000 bugs including ~400 critical). Verified case: **Mythos found 271 of the 423 Firefox flaws fixed by Mozilla in April 2026**, with 3 CVEs explicitly credited to Claude.
- **Restricted, non-commercial distribution**: "We do not plan to make Mythos Preview generally available." Access via **Project Glasswing**: critical infrastructure (energy, water, healthcare, telecoms), extended on June 2, 2026 to ~150 organizations in 15+ countries (including France, Germany, Japan, India, Korea).
- **The strategic reading**: the internal survey of March 2026 (130 employees, median uplift of "4x") concerned **Mythos Preview**, not the commercial Opus, Anthropic thus keeps its most powerful model off the market, distributes it as a quasi-state security instrument, and uses its internal figures as proof of acceleration in its pause appeal. Per the calendar: leak (March 27) → announcement (April 7) → international extension (June 2, the day after the IPO filing) → pause appeal (June 4). One analyst (aibusinessreview, a secondary source to handle with care) sees a "heads I win, tails I win" structure: if Mythos is as dangerous as announced, its leak was negligence; if not, the risk characterization was exaggerated.
- ⚠️ **A boundary to keep watertight**: the April satirical fiction also mentions a "Mythos" that "defies its constitution", that is invented. The real Mythos is a vulnerability-detection tool, not a "rebellious" model.

## 4. The Actions: What Anthropic Does While It Warns

**The financial rocket**: Series F $183B (mid-2025) → Series G $30B at $380B (Feb. 12, 2026, led by GIC and Coatue, with Founders Fund and **MGX, Abu Dhabi**; QIA reported but not confirmed in the official release) → Series H $65B at **$965B** (May 28, 2026) → confidential S-1 (June 1). Run-rate: $4B (July 2025) → $14B (Feb. 2026) → ~$47B (May 2026). First profitable quarter projected for Q2 2026 ($559M operating), with the critics' caveat (Ed Zitron): profit possibly inflated by the SpaceX contract's "ramp" rate, and Anthropic itself warns it will not hold.

**The Gulf memo (July 2025)**: the leaked internal document is the rawest piece in the dossier. Amodei writes, about accepting the petromonarchies' money he had publicly ruled out: "There is a truly giant amount of capital in the Middle East, easily $100B or more. If we want to stay on the frontier, we gain a very large benefit from having access to this capital"; "This is a real downside and I'm not thrilled about it"; and the risk of a "race to the bottom where companies gain a lot of advantage by getting deeper and deeper in bed with the Middle East." Seven months later, MGX co-led the Series G. The author of "Machines of Loving Grace" ("AI-powered authoritarianism seems too terrible to contemplate") documented his own trade-off himself.

**Compute (the physical paradox)**: up to 1 million Google TPUs (>1 GW in 2026, +3.5 GW via Broadcom from 2027); Amazon's Project Rainier (~500,000 Trainium2, >$100B commitment over 10 years, up to 5 GW); and since May 6, 2026, the **exclusive lease of Colossus 1 in Memphis**, the 220,000+ GPUs built by Musk for Grok, with their contested gas turbines (Part 16), leased at ~$15B/year per SpaceX's S-1 (figure disputed). The lab asking for a brake pedal is buying more engine than anyone. Analysts point to the circularity (Amazon invests in Anthropic, which buys Amazon compute, see the upcoming NVIDIA part).

**Defense**: Palantir/AWS (Nov. 2024), Claude Gov (June 2025), DoD contract (July 2025), then the rupture over two red lines (mass surveillance, autonomous weapons), the Hegseth ultimatum, the "supply-chain risk" designation (Feb. 27, 2026), Judge Rita Lin's injunction (March 26: "classic illegal First Amendment retaliation," "Orwellian notion"), the appeal still pending as of June 5, 2026. Nuance: the DoD continued to use Claude for operations (Iran) during the litigation. Net status: neither victory nor capitulation, Anthropic is the only lab with red lines that cost it a $200M contract and a designation as an internal enemy.

**Jobs**: May 2025, Amodei predicts the elimination of ~50% of entry-level white-collar jobs (unemployment up to 20%), on the very day of Claude 4's launch. May 2026: he softens, invoking the Jevons paradox alongside Jamie Dimon ("If you automate 90% of the job, then everyone does the 10%... and kind of 10xs their productivity"). The warning has become a sales argument.

## 5. The Lobbying: Regulatory Capture or Only Adult in the Room?

The facts, which feed both readings:

- **SB 1047 (California, 2024)**: Anthropic first obtained the weakening of the text (removal of the attorney general's power to sue *before* a catastrophe, standard lowered from "reasonable assurance" to "reasonable care," elimination of the dedicated agency, the August 15 amendments incorporated its demands), then supported it half-heartedly ("benefits likely outweigh its costs. However, we are not certain of this"). Newsom vetoed. Anthropic was the only big lab to break the refusal front, but after filing down the bill's teeth.
- **SB 53 (2025)**: Anthropic is the **only big lab to have endorsed** the Transparency in Frontier AI Act (Sept. 8, 2025), signed by Newsom on Sept. 29. Anthropic's argument: "Without it, labs with increasingly powerful models could face growing incentives to dial back their own safety and disclosure programs in order to compete." Critique (Dossier, "The Federal AI Moat"): the text formalizes Anthropic's internal practices as a legal standard, a regulatory moat at the $500M revenue threshold that spares startups but will bind challengers.
- **Constant anti-preemption**: NYT op-ed against the 10-year moratorium (June 2025, "far too blunt"), opposition to the December 2025 preemption EO, **$20M donated to Public First Action** (Feb. 12, 2026), the pro-regulation 501(c)(4), while OpenAI (Brockman), a16z, and Lonsdale fund **Leading the Future** (>$125M, pro-deregulation). Anthropic does not fund Leading the Future. Real gray area: Public First Action has spawned super PACs that spend in federal primaries, and Anthropic claims its donations "weren't intended for federal election spending", the plumbing remains opaque.
- **Federal lobbying**: $280k (2023) → $720k (2024) → ~$3.13M (2025, to be reconfirmed on OpenSecrets). On the other side, the accusers: Sacks ("principally responsible for the state regulatory frenzy," "backdoor Woke AI"), LeCun ("You're being played by people who want regulatory capture"), Gary Marcus (incumbent entrenchment). And Amodei's defense (Oct. 21, 2025): "There are products we will not build and risks we will not take, even if they would make money"; "Startups are among our most important customers."
- **Export controls**: the hardest position in the sector ("It is mortgaging our future as a country to sell these chips to China," "selling nuclear weapons to North Korea"), sincerely geopolitical AND objectively unfavorable to NVIDIA, its non-ally (upcoming part).

## 6. Really Different from OpenAI?

The question deserves a two-column answer rather than a verdict.

**What is structurally identical**: the frontier race (bi-monthly release cadence, ~74 releases in 52 days in early 2026, indicative count); the mega-raises and the IPO ($965B vs. $852B, confidential S-1s a few months apart); Gulf money (MGX in both); lobbying in the millions; defense deals; the justification by inevitability ("if not us, it will be worse"); the shared AGI metaphysics that inflates valuations (Part 14, Part 18). Karen Hao (*Empire of AI*) places both in the same imperial "ends-justify-the-means" logic, while treating Anthropic, her reviewers note, as a more sincere counterpoint than OpenAI.

**What differs, factually**: (1) the two **defense red lines** maintained at the cost of a $200M contract and a legal war with the Pentagon, OpenAI has no documented equivalent; (2) the **SB 53 endorsement** vs. OpenAI's non-position; (3) **Public First Action vs. Leading the Future**, the two labs fund opposing political camps on regulation; (4) the **PBC + Long-Term Benefit Trust** structure (Class T, trustees without equity, climbing toward a board majority) vs. OpenAI's for-profit conversion, noting that the LTBT has "failsafe" clauses actionable by shareholder supermajority, that its Trust Agreement is not public, and that its post-IPO resilience has never been tested; (5) a real relative transparency (publication of alignment faking, detailed system cards, self-caveats) that the FLI Index rewards with a C+… paired with a D in existential safety, like everyone else; (6) the refusal of advertising in Claude ("Ads are coming to AI. But not to Claude," Super Bowl 2026) and revenue built on enterprise rather than consumer.

**The honest synthesis**: the difference exists, it is costly, and it is real, but it operates *inside* the race, never against it. Anthropic is different from OpenAI exactly to the extent that this difference is compatible with a $965B valuation. The February 2026 test (RSP v3.0) showed where the line runs: when the safety commitment conflicts with competitive position, the commitment yields. Jared Kaplan said it plainly: unilateral commitments make no sense "if competitors are blazing ahead." Anthropic's difference is a difference in *conduct within the race*, not a difference about racing.

## 7. The Interpretive Frameworks: Hypocrisy, Marketing, or Structural Trap

Four competing readings, to be held together:

1. **Regulatory capture** (Sacks, a16z, Groq): fear as a competitive-moat strategy, Anthropic's safety standards becoming law, with challengers paying the compliance cost. Strength: SB 1047 weakened *then* supported, SB 53 modeled on its practices, the $500M carve-out. Weakness: the export-controls position and the Pentagon red lines *cost* Anthropic, which a pure capture strategy does not do.
2. **Doom as a sales pitch** (Meyboom, The New Republic): "This technology is the most consequential in human history" functions as "both a caution and an investor's dream"; "We are the responsible ones" = "both a moral claim and a competitive moat." The essay-to-raise timing ("The Adolescence of Technology" → 17 days → $30B) is real. Serious counter-argument: fear would sell better if the risk seemed distant; saying "two years" makes the problem harder to fund, not easier.
3. **The structural trap** (Karnofsky himself, via Torres): "the AGI race isn't a coordination failure", if Anthropic withdrew, the others would say "This is awesome" and barely slow down. So staying is rational. Meyboom: "Rational self-interest, aggregated across all players, produces a collectively irrational and potentially catastrophic outcome." It is not hypocrisy; it is worse: every actor is sincere and the system still produces the race. Torres flips the argument: "the race itself is causing profound harms", individual rationality does not excuse participation.
4. **Epistemic self-sabotage** (Anton Leicht, "Anthropic Against Itself"): the finest framework. By being lab, lobbyist, and whistleblower at once, Anthropic degrades the credibility of its own signals: "your empirical outputs become less credible when linked to a policy agenda." To break through, a warning shot must be "ironclad," yet "empirical findings of a political player are neither." Vicious circle: skepticism "incentivizes Anthropic to put out even more alarming findings." When the real warning comes, who will believe the company that cries wolf with an IPO prospectus in its other hand?

Added to this is the historical comparison Amodei himself wields: not ending up like "the cigarette companies, or the opioid companies, where they knew there were dangers, and they didn't talk about them" (60 Minutes, Nov. 2025). The claimed difference: Anthropic *talks* about the dangers. The question the comparison dodges: would tobacco companies that published their internal studies while continuing to sell have been less culpable, or merely better documented? And the "Oppenheimer trap" (copies of Rhodes on Anthropic desks, an Oppenheimer sticker on a laptop, per Vox): "someone will build it; better it be those who understand the risks", a logic that "holds until it does not."

## 8. Verdict on the Two Axes

On the dossier's two axes (1: accelerate ↔ slow down; 2: concentration ↔ democracy):

- **Axis 1**: Anthropic occupies both poles simultaneously, and that is the central fact, the most "slow down" discourse in the sector (25% p(doom), call for the pause option) carried by the actor that accelerates the most (record cadence, $47B run-rate, compute race). The contradiction's resolution lies in the word "option": Anthropic never asks to slow down *now*, always to be able to slow down *later*, *together*, *if necessary*. The pause is deferred into a conditional future while acceleration occupies the present indicative.
- **Axis 2**: assumed concentration ($965B, Gulf sovereign funds, IPO) with real but untested internal counterweights (LTBT) and a documented preference for regulation, the kind that codifies its practices. The only actor in the dossier to fund the pro-regulation camp ($20M) AND to have paid a real political price (Pentagon) for red lines.
- **The formula**: if Amodei was "the doomer who accelerates" (Part 18), Anthropic in June 2026 is that figure's *institution*, a company whose product is acceleration and whose brand is worry, and whose two discourses are sincere, simultaneous, and structurally irreconcilable. The market, for its part, has ruled: it values the whole, worry included, at 965 billion.
