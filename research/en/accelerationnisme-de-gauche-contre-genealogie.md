## 1. Why This Part

The dossier built a genealogy piece by piece: Marinetti (Part 6), Nick Land (Part 7), Dugin (Part 27), eugenics (Part 28). A right-wing branch, documented. The risk of such a series is a simple objection: you only tell the right-wing story, you charge accelerationism with a sin that is not its own.

This part answers by telling the **fork**. Accelerationism was not born fascist. It came out of a single laboratory, the CCRU at the University of Warwick, and it **split**: on one side Nick Land, toward neoreaction (Part 7); on the other Mark Fisher, toward a left-wing politics. Same trunk, two branches. Showing the fork does two things. It shields the right-wing branch against the charge of guilt by association. And it gives the dossier's "democratize" pole (axis 2, Part 30) the intellectual depth it lacked.

But this is not a rehabilitation. Left accelerationism is here a **lesson, not an icon**. It tried to claim the speed axis (accelerate) in the name of emancipation, and it largely failed: the right realized, as an oligarchy, the acceleration the left dreamed would be emancipatory. This is the dossier's thesis taken from another end. You cannot be for or against speed in the abstract. Everything depends on who owns the accelerator.

## 2. Roots: Marx, Deleuze-Guattari, Lyotard

Before being a movement, accelerationism is a retrospective reading of a small corpus.

- **Marx, the "Fragment on Machines"** (in the *Grundrisse*, written 1857-58). This is the claimed Marxist source: Robin Mackay and Armen Avanessian describe it as Marx's "most overtly accelerationist" writing. The key notion is the ***general intellect***, social knowledge objectified in fixed capital, that is, in machines. The line of reception runs through Italian operaismo and post-operaismo (Negri, *Marx Beyond Marx*, 1978; Virno; Lazzarato) before reaching Srnicek and Williams.
- **Deleuze and Guattari, *Anti-Oedipus*** (1972). This is the matrix sentence, and also the biggest misunderstanding. In the chapter on "the civilized capitalist machine," they write that one must not withdraw from the process but "go further, accelerate the process," a phrase they explicitly attribute to Nietzsche.
- **Lyotard, *Libidinal Economy*** (1974). The great text of the post-68 break with a Marxism judged too structuralist. In a famous passage, Lyotard argues that the English proletariat "enjoyed" (jouissance) its own destruction under industrialization. The passage cost him his former allies at *Socialisme ou Barbarie*.

> **The founding misunderstanding.** The phrase "accelerate the process" from *Anti-Oedipus* is (a) a quotation from Nietzsche, (b) embedded in a dialectic of deterritorialization aiming at a revolutionary overcoming of capital, not an apology for the market. Reading it as a pro-capitalist program is a distortion, popularized above all by Nick Land. Presenting Deleuze and Guattari as "accelerationists" without that caution is already to fall into the trap. Land does not continue Deleuze, he strips the concept of its emancipatory intent.

## 3. The CCRU, and the Land / Fisher Fork

The **Cybernetic Culture Research Unit (CCRU)** forms in late 1995 at Warwick's philosophy department. It is initiated by the cyberfeminist philosopher **Sadie Plant**, then led by **Nick Land** after Plant's departure around 1997. An important point for what follows: it was, originally, an experimental and rather left-leaning milieu, not a Land project.

The collective never had an official institutional existence. After Land left, it could no longer use the premises or claim Warwick's name. A Warwick professor sums it up: "CCRU does not, has not and will never exist." Mark Fisher: "It was never formally disbanded but then again it was never formally constituted." The group practices **theory-fiction**, a hybrid of poststructuralist philosophy, cyberpunk, occultism, and jungle/rave culture. Its most durable concept is **hyperstition** (a portmanteau of *hyper* + *superstition*), coined in 1995: a fiction that, by the mere fact of circulating as an idea, brings about its own realization. A techno-self-fulfilling prophecy.

Gravitating around it, to varying degrees: Nick Land, Sadie Plant, Mark Fisher, Kodwo Eshun, Steve Goodman (Kode9), Reza Negarestani, Iain Hamilton Grant, Ray Brassier, Robin Mackay (now Maya B. Kronic, founder of Urbanomic), Anna Greenspan, Suzanne Livingston, Luciana Parisi. The group dissolves in the early 2000s, after Land's breakdown (amphetamines).

The CCRU is what commentators call "a key progenitor in both left- and right-accelerationist thought." The same trunk produces two branches:

- **Right branch, Nick Land.** The 1990s texts (collected in *Fanged Noumena*, 2011) describe capital as an inhuman intelligence escaping control. After his breakdown and his move to Shanghai, Land reemerges on the far right: the *Dark Enlightenment* series (2013) becomes a founding neoreactionary text, anti-democratic, alongside Curtis Yarvin. All of this is covered in **Part 7**.
- **Left branch, Mark Fisher.** In "Terminator vs. Avatar: Notes on Accelerationism" (2012), Fisher praises Land's analysis but calls his abandonment of the human "fatal." This is the founding gesture of left accelerationism: keep the diagnosis (capital as machine), refuse the conclusion (letting the machine transcend the human).

> **On the word "accelerationism."** It was not coined by its supporters. It is **Benjamin Noys** who forges its contemporary theoretical usage, in *The Persistence of the Negative* (2010), **critically and pejoratively**, to name (in Deleuze-Guattari, Lyotard, Baudrillard) the idea that one would overcome capitalism by intensifying it. The word was, first, a reproach. (Earlier fictional usage: the term "accelerationist" appears in Roger Zelazny's SF novel *Lord of Light*, 1967, in another sense, which Noys acknowledged.)

## 4. Mark Fisher: Capitalist Realism, Hauntology, Acid Communism

**Mark Fisher** (born July 11, 1968 in Leicester) did his PhD at Warwick (1999, *Flatline Constructs: Gothic Materialism and Cybernetic Theory-Fiction*) during the CCRU years. In 2003 he opened the blog **k-punk**, which became a major hub of British cultural criticism. He taught at Goldsmiths, wrote for *The Wire*, co-founded Zero Books and then Repeater Books.

- ***Capitalist Realism: Is There No Alternative?*** (2009, Zero Books). "Capitalist realism" is "the widespread sense that not only is capitalism the only viable political and economic system, but also that it is now impossible even to imagine a coherent alternative to it." An unexpected bestseller (over 10,000 copies for a short theoretical text). The book also ties mental health to politics: a chapter, "The privatisation of stress," calls for repoliticizing mental illness rather than treating it as an individual problem.
- **Hauntology**, developed in ***Ghosts of My Life: Writings on Depression, Hauntology and Lost Futures*** (2014, Zero Books). The term comes from Derrida (*Specters of Marx*). The core of the concept: being haunted not by the past, but by **lost futures**, the promised futures that never arrived. Fisher reads this melancholy in music (Burial, The Caretaker) as much as in politics. Hauntology is not nostalgia: it is mourning for a cancelled future.
- ***Acid Communism***. At his death, Fisher was preparing this book. Only an **unfinished introduction** remains, published in the collection *k-punk: The Collected and Unpublished Writings* (Repeater, 2018). The project: to recover the "lost future" of the 1960s-70s, where a rising class consciousness and a psychedelic counterculture rose together, and to reactivate a collective desire stifled by neoliberalism. Many read it as the sketch of an answer to the "Is There No Alternative?" of *Capitalist Realism*.
- ***Exiting the Vampire Castle*** (November 2013). A polemical essay against a certain online left (the "Vampires' Castle") that would operate through guilt, moral excommunication, and identitarianism, at the expense of class analysis. A much-disputed text: read by some as an early critique of cancel culture, by others as a misstep. Mentioned here without taking sides.

Mark Fisher died by suicide on **January 13, 2017**, at 48, shortly before *The Weird and the Eerie* was published. Depression was both his private struggle and the central object of his work.

> **Three attributions to correct (the difference between a tweet and a dossier).**
> 1. "It is easier to imagine the end of the world than the end of capitalism": Fisher himself attributes it to Jameson and Žižek. The real source is **Fredric Jameson** ("Future City," *New Left Review*, 2003). It is not a Fisher sentence.
> 2. "Slow cancellation of the future": the phrase is **Franco "Bifo" Berardi's** (*After the Future*), which Fisher cites. Not a Fisher coinage.
> 3. "Capitalist realism": the term predated Fisher (German pop artists of the 1960s; Michael Schudson, 1984). Fisher gives it a new meaning, he does not invent it.
> And "Depression is the shadow side of entrepreneurial culture" comes from his *Guardian* article (July 16, 2012), not from the book.

## 5. Left Accelerationism Proper: Srnicek & Williams, Xenofeminism, Bastani

The founding manifesto is **"#Accelerate: Manifesto for an Accelerationist Politics,"** by **Alex Williams and Nick Srnicek**, published on the *Critical Legal Thinking* blog on **May 14, 2013**. Its thesis fits in one distinction: the real dividing line of the left is not capitalism versus anti-capitalism, but a **"folk politics"** (localism, direct action, horizontalism, immediacy) versus an **accelerationist politics** "at ease with a modernity of abstraction, complexity, globality, and technology."

The misreading to avoid is in the manifesto itself: to accelerate is not to go fast. "We may be moving fast, but only within a strictly defined set of capitalist parameters that themselves never waver." The idea is to **repurpose** the technical infrastructure of capitalism and redirect it: "The existing infrastructure is not a capitalist stage to be smashed, but a springboard to launch towards post-capitalism." Hence the avowed call for a "Promethean politics of maximal mastery over society and its environment." Planning, mastery, scale.

The book that develops the manifesto, ***Inventing the Future: Postcapitalism and a World Without Work*** (Srnicek & Williams, Verso, 2015), lays out the positive program: **full automation, universal basic income, reduction of working time, exit from the work ethic**. Its polemical target remains "folk politics," deemed unable to confront global capitalism. A point crucial for this dossier: as of this book, **the authors abandon the word "accelerationism."** Srnicek: "It's been too popularised. And we don't just want everything to go faster, anyway." The "left accelerationism" label is therefore partly retrospective.

Around this core, two satellites:

- **Xenofeminism.** The anonymous collective **Laboria Cuboniks** (six members, the name an anagram of "Nicolas Bourbaki") publishes in 2015 *Xenofeminism: A Politics for Alienation*. Rationalism, anti-naturalism, refusal to let "Nature" serve as political justification: "If nature is unjust, change nature!" **Helen Hester** draws a book from it (*Xenofeminism*, Polity, 2018) summarizing a feminism "technomaterialist, anti-naturalist, and gender abolitionist."
- **Luxury communism.** **Aaron Bastani** (co-founder of Novara Media), *Fully Automated Luxury Communism: A Manifesto* (Verso, 2019), pushes the post-scarcity promise: five "technological drivers" (full automation, near-free energy, asteroid mining, CRISPR, cellular agriculture) answering five crises. To be read knowing its origin as a semi-ironic meme ("Fully Automated Luxury Gay Space Communism"). It is a relative of l/acc, not an avowed identity.

A note on genealogy: the ***#Accelerate Reader*** (Robin Mackay & Armen Avanessian, Urbanomic, 2014) places the two branches side by side, Marx and Land, Lyotard and Srnicek. It is an anthology that **mixes opposed currents**. It should not be read as a left manifesto.

## 6. The Critiques: Noys, Prometheanism, Degrowth

Left accelerationism was criticized from within the left, and that is where it becomes useful to the dossier.

- **Benjamin Noys, *Malign Velocities: Accelerationism and Capitalism*** (Zer0 Books, 2014). For Noys, accelerationism (right and left alike) is a **symptom** of the suffering of labor under capital, and its vice is to dissolve the boundary between radical critique and capitalism: a "radical immanence, of immersion in capital to the point where any way to distinguish a radical strategy from the strategy of capital seems to disappear completely." His prescription is the opposite of speed: negation, the **emergency brake**.
- **The charge of prometheanism.** Several readers (Steven Shaviro, "Accelerationism Without Accelerationism"; Alexander Galloway and his "Brometheanism") note the program's tone of virile mastery, and the reproach of a productivist techno-optimism with an ecological blind spot. The "folk politics" category is itself accused of being a straw man that homogenizes very diverse activist practices.
- **Degrowth, the opposite wager.** At the other end of the speed axis, but still on the left: Jason Hickel, *Less is More: How Degrowth Will Save the World* (2020), for whom "green growth" is a fantasy with no empirical support, growth being tied to resource use. And Kohei Saito, *Marx in the Anthropocene* (2023) / *Slow Down* (2024), who reads in the late Marx's ecological notebooks an abandonment of productivism in favor of a "degrowth communism." (To be presented as a Saito thesis: his "degrowth Marx" is a hotly contested philological interpretation, not an established fact.)

> **The internal fault line.** The left thus divides on the dossier's **axis 1 (accelerate or slow down)**: l/acc on one side, degrowth on the other. Yet both camps share the same neglect of **axis 2 (concentration)**. It is the exact mirror of the doom-versus-e/acc debate analyzed in the synthesis: a dispute over speed that leaves the question of power untouched.

## 7. The 2026 Bridge: Accelerate What, Owned by Whom

The l/acc dream was to accelerate technology toward post-work. Thirteen years after the manifesto, the lock is visible: **the accelerator belongs to an oligarchy.** According to Epoch AI (measured in H100-equivalents, published April 2026), the five largest hyperscalers (Amazon, Google, Meta, Microsoft, Oracle) hold **71% of global AI compute in Q4 2025, up from 63% in early 2024**. The promise of an emancipatory technology runs into the fact that the means to produce it are among the most concentrated that exist (see Part 16).

The irony is complete: **it is the right that realized the acceleration.** Andreessen and Beff Jezos's "effective accelerationism" (e/acc, Parts 1, 4) is its mainstream version, and the 2024 US election put it in power (tech capital behind Trump, David Sacks as "AI czar," a deregulatory agenda). The left lost the speed axis precisely because it had underestimated the power axis: you do not accelerate toward emancipation when you do not own the machine.

The contemporary heirs that do tackle axis 2 (ownership):

- **Public interest AI.** Chinasa T. Okolo, "The paradox of AI accelerationism and the promise of public interest AI" (*Science*, 2025), opposes to concentration an AI that "distributes technological control among diverse stakeholders… rather than concentrating power among a few private firms," and places e/acc within the TESCREAL ideological frame (Part 28). The **Public AI** movement (White Paper on Public AI, 2024; Public AI Network; CalCompute in California; the European *AI Factories*) proposes to **socialize the "means of prediction"** (data, compute, expertise), by direct analogy with the means of production. It is the l/acc insight finally plugged into the right axis: not just accelerate, but collectively own the accelerator.
- **Technodiversity.** Yuk Hui (Stiegler's student, already in Part 19) refuses the binary choice accelerate/slow. His **cosmotechnics** "is always local": the point is to **diversify** technologies, rooted in plural cosmologies, rather than to accelerate or brake a single one assumed to be universal.

## 8. What It Brings to the Dossier

Three precise contributions.

1. **The genealogy is no longer one-sided.** The CCRU fork proves accelerationism was not fascist in essence: Land and Fisher start from the same point. This strengthens Part 7 rather than weakening it, because it answers in advance the objection "you only document the right." Land's fascism is a choice, not a fate of the concept.

2. **Proof by failure, on both axes.** Left accelerationism tried to claim axis 1 (accelerate) while standing on the right side of axis 2 (democratize). It failed because it believed speed would be enough. The right captured speed by owning the compute. The lesson is consistent with the dossier's thesis: **for or against speed is the wrong debate; the real question is who owns the accelerator.**

3. **The "democratize" pole now has a lineage.** Fisher names the disease (capitalist realism, the impossibility of imagining an alternative). The candidate cures, in turn, are ways of **making a limit opposable** or **redistributing the machine**: Noys's emergency brake and degrowth (Hickel, Saito), public AI and the socialization of compute, Yuk Hui's technodiversity. This is the intellectual matter that was missing from Parts 25 (solutions) and 30 (the democratize counter-pole).

> And a warning, so as not to idealize. The "Promethean politics of maximal mastery" of the 2013 manifesto is a **left-wing version of the limitless**, that same refusal of any limit that the dossier diagnoses as the central danger. Noys's critique (the brake) and degrowth are the left's answer to its own temptation. In other words: the limitless is not the right's monopoly, and it is by giving itself limits, not by accelerating, that the left distinguishes itself from what it fights.
