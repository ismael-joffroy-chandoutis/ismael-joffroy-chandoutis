## 1. The Real Footprint: The 2026 Figures

- **Electricity**: datacenters ≈ 415 TWh (2024) → ~485 TWh (2025) → **~945-950 TWh projected in 2030** (~3% of global demand), according to the IEA ("Energy and AI," 2025). Datacenter demand grew by +17% in 2025; *AI* datacenters by **+50%**. ⚠️ The much-cited figure "1,000 TWh as early as 2026" comes from the IEA's January 2024 report (including crypto); later editions push that threshold toward 2030, cite it with the date of the report.
- **The real mix**: ~60% of the electricity physically consumed by datacenters comes from fossil fuels (coal ~30%), 27% renewables, 15% nuclear (IEA). The "contractual mix" of green PPAs displayed by the hyperscalers is an accounting device, not a physical reality. A revealing signal: **+70% of gas turbine orders in 2025**.
- **Hyperscaler emissions**: Microsoft +23.4% since 2020 (the "carbon negative 2030" goal maintained on paper; a postponement reportedly under consideration, May 2026, unconfirmed); Google +~50% over five years. The accounting sleight of hand is documented: in "market-based" terms (credits), Google reports -12%; in "**location-based**" terms (physical), its Scope 2 emissions go from 5.8 to 11.2 Mt CO₂ (2020-2024), Microsoft's from 4.3 to ~10 Mt. The "decrease" is an artifact of carbon credits.
- **Water**: ~560 billion liters consumed by datacenters in 2023 (IEA); UN projection (UNU/UNEP) of up to 9.3 trillion liters in 2030 if AI reaches 40% of datacenter electricity consumption, very wide ranges, opacity from operators (Kaveh Madani, UNU: "AI is also physical infrastructure").
- **Materials and waste**: 2-3% of global demand for copper and critical minerals by 2030, in direct competition with the energy transition; ~62 Mt/year of global e-waste, marginal recycling of infrastructure.
- **France**: GreenIT estimates AI at 41 Mt CO₂e in 2025, a projection of ×7 by 2030, described as "unsustainable" (medium confidence).

## 2. The Return of Limits to Growth

The Meadows report (1972) is enjoying a scientific second life directly relevant to AI:

- **Herrington (2021, Journal of Industrial Ecology)** compares the World3 model to 2000-2020 data: the scenarios that fit best are **BAU2** (resources doubled) and **CT** (comprehensive technology). Yet BAU2 still leads to overshoot-and-collapse, triggered not by resource depletion but by **pollution**, peaking ~2040. Translation: doubling resources and recycling does not change the mode, it shifts the cause of collapse.
- **Nebel et al. (2024)** recalibrate World3 on empirical data: the BAU scenario remains the one that deviates least. (⚠️ a distinct recalibration, not to be attributed to Herrington.)
- **The application to AI**: in the grammar of World3, AI is a textbook case of "Comprehensive Technology", the technology that pushes back one limit (efficiency) by accelerating the approach of another (pollution, energy, materials). Meadows's CT scenario does not collapse from a lack of resources but from the rising costs of the technology itself. The hyperscalers' $400B of annual capex (+75% expected in 2026) is literally that cost.

## 3. Jevons: The Paradox Claimed by the Hyperscalers Themselves

The most remarkable fact of the DeepSeek sequence (January 2025): confronted with a model 10× cheaper, the market panicked for a day, then **Satya Nadella tweeted**: "Jevons paradox strikes again! As AI gets more efficient and accessible, we will see its use skyrocket." Meta raised its 2025 capex to $60-65B (+50%). The Jevons paradox (1865: efficiency increases total consumption) went from being an environmentalist objection to being an official **investment argument**. Amodei invokes it on employment (Part 20), Nadella on energy, the same figure serves to promise that nothing will ever slow down.

The academic version (Luccioni et al. ACM FAccT 2025): efficiency gains (Koomey's law) are crushed by the volume of compute; rebound effects make the impacts "structural and not marginal." This is the formal junction with Limits to Growth: efficiency without a ceiling is an accelerator, not a brake. Hence the useful French distinction between sufficiency and efficiency: **digital sufficiency** (Parrique et al.) questions the use upstream, up to renunciation, "frugal AI" being judged an intrinsic contradiction.

## 4. The "AI for Climate" Record: 74% of Claims Unproven

The two pieces of the dossier, to be held together:

- **For the prosecution**: the report by the Beyond Fossil Fuels consortium (Ketan Joshi, February 17, 2026, ahead of the New Delhi summit) analyzes 154 claims of AI climate benefits: **74% unproven**, 36% with no citation at all, and **not a single example** where ChatGPT/Gemini/Copilot produces "material, verifiable, and substantial" emissions reductions. A structuring distinction: the documented benefits come from "traditional" AI (targeted ML, power grids, weather), the harms from *generative* AI. Greenwashing consists precisely of conflating the two. Greenpeace East Asia ranks NVIDIA **last** in the AI supply chain on decarbonization (chip manufacturing emissions ×4.5 in one year).
- **For the defense (conditional)**: the Grantham/LSE + Systemiq study (*npj Climate Action*, June 2025): AI *could* reduce emissions by 3.2-5.4 Gt CO₂e/year by 2035 (grids, food, transport), more than the rise in datacenter consumption. But the authors' own caveat is the key: this requires targeted public investment, shared data, equitable access; "without active policy, commercial incentives will not steer AI toward the socially useful uses." And the real textbook case exists: **GraphCast** (DeepMind) forecasts the weather at 10 days in <1 min on a TPU, ~1000× less energy than numerical forecasting, traditional AI, trained once, run often.
- **The honest synthesis**: the "AI will save the climate" narrative leans on a conditional potential to 2035 (Grantham) to cover an observed negative record in 2026 (BFF). The two studies do not contradict each other: one describes what would be possible with a public policy that no one is pursuing, the other describes what is actually happening.

## 5. Nuclear Power: The 2026 Inventory, Deal by Deal

**The deals (all real, all distant)**:

| Deal | Content | Real timeline |
|---|---|---|
| Microsoft / Constellation | 20-year PPA, restart of Three Mile Island Unit 1 ("Crane Clean Energy Center," 835 MW). Constellation investment **$1.6B** (⚠️ the "$16B" that circulates is the contribution to Pennsylvania's GDP, not the deal) + $1B DOE federal loan | 2027-2028 |
| Google / Kairos | Up to 500 MW of SMR (6-7 reactors) | First reactor ~2030, fleet 2035 |
| Amazon / X-energy | $700M invested, up to 12 Xe-100 reactors + Susquehanna $20B campus | Dow/Texas FID ~2028, deployment 2030s |
| Meta | The largest commitment: 20-year Clinton PPA (1,121 MW, June 2027) + Vistra/Oklo/TerraPower up to 6.6 GW | 2027 (existing) to 2035 (new) |

By spring 2026, 13 projects commit >9.8 GW; Amazon, Google, and Meta have signed the pledge to triple global nuclear capacity by 2050.

**The real state of SMRs**: **no Western commercial SMR in service in early 2026.** NuScale: the only NRC-certified design, but its flagship project (Idaho) canceled in 2023 over costs (~$89/MWh), reactor revenue not before the 2030s. Oklo (chaired by Sam Altman before his move into its capital): a 14 GW pipeline, pre-revenue. TerraPower (Gates): Natrium pushed to 2030+, a transversal bottleneck: the shortage of **HALEU** fuel. X-energy: IPO April 2026 ($1.02B raised), final investment decision ~2028. LCOE of first-of-a-kind units: $90-160/MWh, above wind/solar. **The only exception is Chinese**: Linglong One (ACP100, Hainan) targeted commercial operation in H1 2026, not confirmed as grid-connected at the date of this report. China has 29 reactors under construction, ~half the global total.

**Fusion**: no installation in the world produces net commercial electricity. ITER will *never* produce electricity (no turbine), its D-T schedule slipped to 2039. Helion (Altman) signed the world's first fusion PPA (Microsoft, 50 MW, "2028," with penalties); CFS/SPARC targets net energy in 2027 and a plant in Virginia in the early 2030s (PPA >$1B with Eni). ETH Zurich analysis (Nature Energy, 2026): learning rates too optimistic, fusion may not compete with solar+batteries before 2040. To be treated as a bet, not a plan.

**The fundamentals of the substantive debate**:
- **Cost** (Lazard LCOE+ 2025, unsubsidized): onshore wind $37-86/MWh, utility solar $38-217, gas CCGT $48-109, **nuclear $141-220**. Solar/wind the cheapest for the 10th consecutive year.
- **Timelines** (the EPR as a warning): Flamanville 3 at full power in December 2025, ~12 years late, ~€23.7B (vs. €3.3B planned). Hinkley Point C: £36-40B (vs. £18B). Olkiluoto-3: 14 years late. France's EPR2 program: ceiling revised to **€72.8B** (+40% vs. 2022), first concrete 2029, commissioning **2038**.
- **Safety** (Our World in Data): coal ~24.6 deaths/TWh, gas ~3, **nuclear <0.1**, like solar and wind. Nuclear kills 99.8% less than coal. On this criterion, the debate is settled.
- **Waste**: no permanent geological repository has ever opened in the world. The first, Onkalo (Finland), is awaiting its operating license (STUK decision ~late June 2026); Cigéo (Bure) targets a pilot phase in 2035. "Eternal pollutant" is rhetorically true (promised containment: 100,000 years, unverifiable by construction) but the volume is minuscule compared to the fossil waste dispersed in the atmosphere, the asymmetry of the two pollutions (concentrated/managed vs. diffuse/endured) is the honest core of the debate.
- **Uranium**: a spike in early 2026 (>$100/lb), demand +28% by 2030; the WNA/IAEA consensus is that the constraint is underinvestment, not geology (sources partly interested, Sprott et al.).

## 6. The Nuclear Verdict: Neither Mirage nor Solution, a Temporal Alibi

The answer to the question posed ("mirage? eternal pollutant or genuinely good?") comes in four documented propositions:

1. **On the merits, nuclear is defensible**: low-carbon, dispatchable, safe (<0.1 death/TWh), with a real but managed waste question on a scale incommensurable with fossil fuels. The EU green taxonomy classified it as transitional (the parliamentary veto failed: 278 for, 328 against) and the CJEU confirmed it. The historic environmentalist split (Germany out in 2023... while increasing its gas) belongs more to political history than to physics.
2. **On timing, it is a structural mismatch**: AI demand arrives in 3-5 years, new nuclear delivers in 10-15 (EPR2: 2038; SMRs: early 2030s at best; fusion: speculative). The IEA is explicit: nuclear only plays a role "at the end of the decade and beyond"; until then, renewables ~half the additional demand, then **gas and coal**. MIT Tech Review: even while touting nuclear, the tech companies "will rely heavily on fossil fuels, keep coal plants open, build gas that will remain for decades."
3. **Therefore the real function of the nuclear deals in 2024-2026 is narrative**: a pipeline of *conditional* agreements on an unproven-at-scale technology (25 GW end of 2024 → 45 GW mid-2026) serves as a decarbonization narrative while the real expansion runs on gas (Memphis, +70% of turbines ordered) and coal (China). The hierarchy of credibility: **restarts (near-certain, 2027-28) > new nuclear (2030s) > SMRs (industrial bets) > fusion (a scientific bet)**. Only the restarts (TMI, Clinton) are probable megawatts on a useful horizon.
4. **The synthetic figure**: Sam Altman chairs the double bet (fission Oklo + fusion Helion), AI finances the energy that will justify AI. Fatih Birol (IEA) offers the techno-optimist framing ("AI is no longer just an energy taker, it is becoming an energy maker"); the left-wing split is documented between ecomodernists (Breakthrough Institute, Monbiot, Brand, "the only demonstrated dispatchable zero-carbon technology") and ecosocialists (Joshua Frank, Spectre: "false solution"). Working position for this dossier: nuclear is a good answer to the wrong question. The question is not "how to power the infinite growth of compute" but "what volume of compute is justified, and for what", and that is exactly the question the nuclear deals serve to avoid asking.

## 7. Degrowth, Sufficiency, Moratoriums: The Counter-Position

- **The intellectual corpus**: Hickel ("exnovation": reallocating productive capacities), Saito (*Slow Down*, 500k+ copies sold, "locking technology," capitalist efficiency as an overproduction machine), Parrique (digital sufficiency). ⚠️ Documentary honesty: neither Saito nor Parrique has a published position *specifically* on AI verified by this research, their frameworks apply to it, but direct attribution would be abusive. "Digital degrowth" is an emerging corpus, not a stabilized one.
- **The practice**: it is the local terrain that carries the real counter-position, the Irish moratorium (2021, lifted Dec. 2025 under conditions: on-site generation, 80% renewable), durable Dutch restrictions (hyperscale ≥70 MW confined), **Virginia** (≥25 datacenters canceled in 2025, ~4× 2024; Loudoun County removes "by-right" zoning; 60+ bills in 2026 including a moratorium until 2028; only 35% of voters favorable to new datacenters), Memphis (NAACP lawsuit, Part 21). Data Center Watch: ~$64-100B of projects blocked/delayed (medium confidence).
- **The structured political demand**: the "**Within Bounds**" declaration (130+ organizations, Paris summit, February 2025), "fossil-free" AI infrastructure and resource ceilings compatible with planetary limits. This is the political translation of Meadows: not "better," but "within bounds."
- **The point of junction with the rest of the dossier**: Ireland (22% of national electricity in datacenters) ultimately reopened under conditions, a demonstration that even moratoriums that hold for four years yield to capex pressure. Without a *global* ceiling (compute cap, energy conditionality), local victories displace datacenters more than they stop them. The question of solutions is addressed in Part 25.
