## 1. Why this part

The previous parts describe an **ascendant tech-right coalition**. But no power exists without a counter-power. This report surveys **who, concretely, can rein in or rebalance** this dynamic, and with what real effectiveness.

---

## 2. Europe: the principal institutional counter-power

The EU has established itself as **the only regulatory bloc operating at scale** against the tech giants, through three pieces of legislation:
- **AI Act**, the first comprehensive legal framework on AI (a risk-based approach).
- **DSA (Digital Services Act)**, platform accountability (illegal content, transparency, minors).
- **DMA (Digital Markets Act)**, ex ante competition law targeting the **"gatekeepers"**: opening up app stores, ending the imposed 15-30% commissions, interoperability.

**Enforcement 2025-2026 (ramping up):**
- Formal investigations against **Apple, Google, Meta, Amazon, Microsoft**.
- December: an antitrust investigation into **Google's use of content to train its AI** and its "AI Overviews"; an investigation into **Meta/WhatsApp** (access for rival AIs).
- 2026: Brussels **tightens** enforcement, while **Trump threatens retaliation** and **Vance** denounces European regulation as an attack on freedom and dynamism.

> The EU is therefore the **main front**, but under direct American **geopolitical pressure**.

---

## 3. American antitrust

- A wave of **antitrust lawsuits** (driven notably by **Lina Khan** at the FTC, a legacy of the previous administration) targeting Google, Amazon, Meta, Apple.
- A direct test of Thiel's **doctrine of the embraced monopoly** ("competition is for losers").
- **Fragility**: the orientation of antitrust depends heavily on the executive; under an administration allied with the tech-right, its intensity may **recede**.

## 4. Intellectual critiques

- **Cory Doctorow**, the concept of **"enshittification"** (*Enshittification*, Verso, 2025): the inexorable degradation of platforms (first good for users, then for business customers, then only for themselves). He argues for **interoperability** and the end of *lock-in* as structural remedies.
- **Timnit Gebru & Émile P. Torres**, the **TESCREAL** critique: deconstructing the ideological "bundle" and its eugenicist roots.
- **Adam Tooze, Henry Farrell, Jacobin, The Conversation**, critiques of reactionary elitism and of the techno-capitalist "faith" (cf. Part 1).
- **Yanis Varoufakis**, the **"technofeudalism"** thesis: capitalism would mutate into a feudalism of platforms (rent, digital vassalage).
- **Shoshana Zuboff**, **"surveillance capitalism"**: behavioral data as the raw material of an unprecedented power.
- **Mark Fisher** (legacy), **left** accelerationism, the antithesis of Land.

---

## 5. Public opinion and social movements

- **Massive distrust**: **Ipsos** polls, > **70%** of Americans want AI safety standards; **83%** do **not trust** AI companies to self-regulate.
- **Anti-AI backlash (2026)**: a rise in hostile sentiment, going as far as **violent incidents** (according to *Fortune*), a sign of popular rejection that can carry political weight.
- **Mobilizations**: unions (Hollywood, voice dubbing, journalism), teachers, artists (copyright vs. model training), and the **"trust & safety"** community explicitly targeted by Andreessen.

---

## 6. Internal fault lines as a counter-power

The tech-right camp is **not monolithic**, its fractures are an endogenous brake:
- **e/acc vs. effective altruism** (accelerate vs. secure AI): Bostrom, Yudkowsky, and part of **Anthropic** (Amodei) pull toward **caution**.
- **Altman/OpenAI outside Thiel's orbit**: an independent AI pole.
- **Reid Hoffman**: funds the **Democratic camp**.
- **Crypto decentralization vs. AI concentration**: an internal contradiction (cf. Part 4).
- **Karp** (Habermas, "socialist, not woke") diverges from the neo-reactionaries.

A **major AI accident** would instantly rearm the camp of caution (scenario C of Part 5).

---

## 7. Limits and fragilities

1. **Asymmetry of resources**: the tech-right coalition combines capital, infrastructure, and state access; the counter-powers are **dispersed**.
2. **Capture of the American state**: when the executive is allied with the tech-right, antitrust and domestic regulation **weaken**.
3. **Pressure on Europe**: US trade threats, the risk of regulatory fragmentation.
4. **Technological dependence**: it is hard to regulate infrastructures one depends on (cloud, AI, payments).
5. **Speed**: technology advances faster than the law (the wager openly made by accelerationism).

> **Bottom line:** the most tangible counter-power remains **Europe** (regulation); the most powerful in the long run would be an **alignment between distrustful public opinion, antitrust, and internal fractures**, triggered by a shock (an AI accident, a crypto crash, a surveillance scandal).
