> Synthesis report. Written June 5, 2026. Draws on the entire dossier (Parts 1-24), main sources at the end of the document.
> Part 25: the dossier documented the problem across 24 parts. This part answers the question that remains: **what can be done so that the planet does not go down with AI?** Method: retain only levers with either documented proof of effectiveness, or a credible mechanism and identified carriers. No wishful thinking, no fatalism — a portfolio.

## 1. Framing: bifurcate from what?

"That the earth does not go down with AI" covers four distinct threats, documented in the dossier, which call for different levers — conflating them produces answers that miss everything:

| Threat | Parts | Nature |
|---|---|---|
| **A. Concentration of power** (private US oligarchy / state techno-authoritarianism) | 2, 8, 15, 20, 23 | Political |
| **B. Planetary limits** (energy, water, materials, emissions) | 16, 22 | Physical |
| **C. Dislocation of work and income** | 18, 21 | Social |
| **D. Epistemic corrosion** (industrialized differend, undecidability) | 13, 19, 24 | Cognitive |

Existential risk in the Yudkowsky manner (Part 14) is not retained as an operational category: not because it is impossible, but because its proposed remedies (a global moratorium at 8 GPUs) are the least actionable in the field, and because — the conclusion of Part 14 — it is precisely variance and uncertainty that justify prudence, and prudence runs through levers A-D.

## 2. What has already worked: the proofs of effectiveness

Before the proposals, the inventory of what has *already* produced measurable effects — the empirical basis for hope:

1. **Labor law seizes the algorithm**: the EU platform directive (2024) is enforceable — presumption of employment, right to explanation, collective bargaining over algorithmic management. South Korea's Yellow Envelope Act (183-3, March 2026) opens unionization to gig workers: ~100,000 bargaining requests in 2 days. (Part 21)
2. **The collective contract draws lines**: SAG-AFTRA obtained through 320 days of strike what no law imposed (consent, disclosure, ban on capturing future consent at hiring). The Teamsters have a contract with a mandatory human operator. (Part 21)
3. **The ground blocks the concrete**: ≥25 data centers cancelled in Virginia in 2025, ~$64-100B of projects blocked or delayed (Data Center Watch), Irish (4 years) and Dutch moratoriums, NAACP lawsuit against xAI. Documented limit: Ireland reopened under conditions — the local delays, it does not stop on its own. (Parts 21, 22)
4. **American federalism held**: the federal moratorium on state laws defeated 99-1 in the Senate (July 2025) by a cross-partisan coalition; the December 2025 preemption EO cannot legally invalidate any state law. SB 53 is in force in California. (Parts 17, 20, 21)
5. **The court remains a court**: Judge Lin's injunction against Anthropic's "supply-chain risk" designation ("Orwellian notion") proves the executive still loses in court. The Google case (2025 antitrust remedy), the SAMR/Autorité de la concurrence/DOJ investigations into NVIDIA. (Parts 17, 20, 23)
6. **Forced transparency changes the numbers**: location-based accounting demolished Google's and Microsoft's net-zero narrative; the "74% unproven" report flipped the burden of proof on "AI for climate"; the "concentration of power" chapter of the International AI Safety Report 2026 (Bengio) institutionalizes the dossier's axis 2. (Parts 18, 22)
7. **The state can discipline platforms when it wants to**: China proved it ("moderate algorithm" rules, in-app hour caps, 996 ruled illegal) — a disturbing proof of existence: hard algorithmic regulation is *technically possible*; what is missing in the West is the balance of power, not feasibility. (Parts 15, 21)

## 3. The toolbox, lever by lever

### Lever 1 — Break the concentration (threat A)
- **Offensive antitrust**: ban or unwind "acqui-hires" (Meta-Scale, NVIDIA-Groq, Google-Windsurf — Warren/Wyden/Blumenthal letter); mandate **disclosure of circular financing** (the missing metric of Part 23: % of NVIDIA revenue coming from entities it finances); structurally, separate the layers (compute / models / applications) as electricity generation and distribution were once separated. Carriers: FTC/DOJ post-2028, DG COMP, SAMR. Realism: medium — but the Google case proves the machinery exists.
- **Ban the bailout preemptively**: the Warren position ("privatize the profits, socialize the losses") — legislate *before* the bubble bursts so that OpenAI's/the neoclouds' losses are not socialized. Realism: low before the crisis, high during. To be prepared now (texts ready).
- **Condition public procurement**: the state is the biggest customer — condition public purchase of AI on transparency (consumption, training data, labor), a lever requiring no new law. Carriers: EU (AI Act procurement articles), cities, blue US states.

### Lever 2 — Cap the footprint (threat B)
- **Energy conditionality** (the Irish model generalized): every data center must bring its own *additional* decarbonized generation + grid give-back + real-time water/energy transparency. This is the realistic version of the moratorium: not "no," but "not without paying the true cost." Carriers: energy regulators (Ireland's CRU did it), 60+ bills in Virginia, the Ross bill in France.
- **Caps within limits** ("Within Bounds," 130+ NGOs): sector-level carbon/water budgets for digital, enforceable. Realism: low at 5 years globally, real at local/national level (Ireland and the Netherlands did it de facto).
- **Tax the AI rent**: data center tax (Warren), AI tax financing a just transition (Díaz/EPSCO), robot tax (Sanders). The finest version articulates B and C: tax mass inference compute (the volume) rather than research. Realism: medium — Spain is carrying it at the European level.
- **Mandate location-based accounting**: prohibit companies from reporting emissions in market-based terms alone. Nearly free, devastating for greenwashing, enforceable through the European CSRD. Realism: high.

### Lever 3 — Protect labor and redistribute (threat C)
- **Extend the platform directive to all employees** (the ETUC's "AI at work" demand): right to explanation, ban on fully automated decisions (74% of Europeans in favor), mandatory consultation before deployment. This is the extension of an existing gain, not a utopia. Realism: high within 5 years.
- **The SAG-AFTRA model generalized**: consent + disclosure + remuneration for any replica/training data derived from work. Carriers: creative unions worldwide, the Japanese "voice rights" panel, the 2026 negotiations.
- **Codetermination**: the Sanders plan (45% of board seats, the German model) — the only proposal that gives workers power over *deployment* and not merely compensation after the fact.
- **Prepare redistribution**: if Amodei's predictions (20% unemployment) are even half right, the window for building the mechanisms (32-hour week, transition funds, data dividend) is *before* the wave. The history in this dossier (Part 21) shows the left wins on existing institutional gains: build the institution now.

### Lever 4 — Repair the epistemic (threat D)
- **Transparency of recommender systems** (the DSA, actually enforced): enforceable algorithm audits, right to a chronological feed, interoperability. The DSA exists — enforcement is the fight (Part 10).
- **Public funding of informational commons**: the press, Wikipedia, archives — the "common tribunals" that the industrialized differend (Part 24) dissolves. Inexpensive compared to a single data center.
- **Public AI as epistemic infrastructure**: EuroStack (Bria/Mazzucato), public data centers (LFI), non-agentic Scientist AI (LawZero/Bengio, >$100M in Canadian public funding under discussion), India AI Mission, open models à la BLOOM. Limit documented in Part 21: "an intellectual horizon without means at scale" — the political decision that would change everything: a CERN for AI (Hassabis proposes it, Part 18) funded the way CERN actually was.
- **The factitious aesthetic** (Chatonsky, Part 24): at the cultural level, refusing the models' default referential regime — the artists' role is not decorative in this portfolio: it is the only lever that operates *inside* latent space.

### Lever 5 — Compute governance (transversal)
The field's most structural proposal, carried beyond the doomers (RAND, GovAI, the Safety Report's concentration chapter): compute is AI's only input that is physical, measurable, locatable and already oligopolistic (TSMC/ASML/NVIDIA) — hence the only realistic point of democratic control. Versions: compute registries (who owns what), notification thresholds (the EU and SB 53 already have them), conditionality (access to public compute in exchange for obligations), and eventually negotiated caps. This is the actionable version of what Yudkowsky's treaty (Part 14) fantasizes in police form. Realism: the building blocks exist (export controls = proof that compute IS governable — simply, today, solely in the service of US-China rivalry).

## 4. The four bifurcation scenarios, 2026-2030

What can actually deflect the current trajectory? Four mechanisms, not mutually exclusive:

1. **The bubble bursts** (non-negligible probability — Burry, BIS, IMF worried; 4% of revenue for 100% of capex, Part 23). Ambivalent effects: it purges speculation, BUT concentrates further (the survivors buy up the rest), socializes the losses if nothing is prepared, and discredits regulation ("the market solved the problem"). **The useful bifurcation requires anti-bailout and antitrust texts ready BEFORE the crisis** — the lesson of 2008, missed.
2. **The physical constraint bites** (near-certain in places): grid connection queues (Dublin, Northern Virginia, Frankfurt), water (Chile, Querétaro), turbine shortages (+70% in orders). Physics is the only regulator lobbying cannot buy. The useful bifurcation: turn every bottleneck into conditionality (the Irish model) rather than into a waiver (the Memphis model).
3. **Regulation holds and spreads** (ongoing, fragile): the AI Act enforced despite the Omnibus, the DSA, the platform directive transposed (Dec. 2026), SB 53 + state laws surviving the courts, Brazil/Korea/Japan legislating. The decisive battle is **preemption** (on both sides of the Atlantic): as long as the lower levels can act, regulation advances by example.
4. **The social movement converges** (the most uncertain, the most powerful): the junction documented in Part 21 between unions (jobs), local communities (water/air), creators (consent) and the public (70% worried) has not yet had its federating moment. Historical precedents (tobacco, CFCs/ozone, civil nuclear after TMI) show that an "inevitable" industry can be constrained in ~a decade when the narrative flips. The "74% unproven" report, the deaths of Boxtown and the models' paywall are the materials of such a flip.

## 5. What will probably not work (and why)

The portfolio's honesty requires the negative list:
- **The global moratorium** (Part 14): no enforcement mechanism, guaranteed defection, and the only entity talking about it (Anthropic, Part 20) conditions it on US-China verification that does not exist. Real function: rhetorical.
- **The appeal to self-regulation**: Anthropic's RSP — the sector's most serious commitment — was abandoned as soon as it got in the way (February 2026). QED.
- **Consumer boycott alone**: AI is B2B and infrastructural; the consumer is not the customer, the consumer is the input.
- **"Just vote"**: necessary, insufficient — both US parties are funded by the same AI super PACs (>$321M in the 2026 cycle, Part 20), and the EU retreats via Omnibus under a pro-regulation Parliament. Without organized counter-power between elections, the vote ratifies.
- **Waiting for the "good" model** (the aligned AI that will solve AI): the secular version of the eschatology documented in Part 4. No mechanism.

## 6. Synthesis: the portfolio

The answer to "how to keep the earth from going down" is not ONE solution but a portfolio across three horizons:

- **Now (0-2 years, existing building blocks)**: enforce the DSA/AI Act without gutting them; transpose the platform directive; mandatory location-based accounting; Irish-style energy conditionality; anti-bailout and anti-acqui-hire texts ready; support local litigation (NAACP) and strikes over AI; compute registries.
- **Next (2-5 years, balances of power to build)**: an extended "AI at work" directive; an AI/data-center tax financing the transition (the Díaz model); structural separation of compute/models/applications; massive public funding of public AI (CERN scale, not subsidy scale); codetermination.
- **Horizon (5-10 years, regime change)**: international governance of compute (registries → thresholds → negotiated caps); material budgets for digital within planetary limits; the cultural narrative flipped — AI treated like aviation or medicine: useful, regulated, insurable, accountable, *boring*.

**The synthesis sentence for the whole dossier**: the danger is not that AI is too powerful; it is that its current development requires — economically, energetically, politically — that no limit be enforceable against it. Each lever in this part is a way of making a limit enforceable. The earth does not go down with AI; it goes down with the limitless. And the limitless, for its part, has addresses, balance sheets and building permits — which is to say, handles.
