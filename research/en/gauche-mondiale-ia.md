# The Global Left and AI: Outpaced or Alternative?

> Documented research report. Written June 5, 2026. Sources at the end of the document.

> Key claims verified through adversarial counter-research (corrections incorporated, see methodological notes).

## 1. United States: A Structured Pole, a Divided Strategy

**Sanders, the class-war framing.** On October 6, 2025, Bernie Sanders publishes "The Big Tech Oligarchs' War Against Workers": AI/automation could eliminate ~100 million US jobs in 10 years (40% of nurses, 47% of truckers, 64% of accountants, 89% of fast-food workers). Five-point plan: **robot tax**, **32-hour workweek with no loss of pay** (Thirty-Two Hour Workweek Act), 20% of capital distributed to employees + 45% of board seats (the German codetermination model), a ban on union busting and stock buybacks. Real methodological caveat: the job estimates, partly compiled via ChatGPT/O*NET, are criticized as speculative (AEI) — to be read as a quantified political framing, not a forecast. In March 2026, Sanders and AOC introduce the **AI Data Center Moratorium Act** (moratorium on datacenters >20 MW, lifted only if a federal law guarantees protections for workers, consumers, the environment, and civil rights).

**Khanna, the bridge.** Ro Khanna (Silicon Valley's representative) publishes his "democratic AI" doctrine (The Nation, April 2026): human in the loop, collective bargaining, tax-code reform (which subsidizes the robot against the worker) + a "data dividend," a New Deal-inspired "Future Workforce Administration" (1M "Work for America" jobs), datacenters serving communities, a federal AI agency. He explicitly refuses the "Luddite complete ban" — a bridge position between the left and the tech pragmatists.

**Warren, the antitrust arm.** Investigations (with Wyden) into cloud-AI partnerships (Microsoft/OpenAI, Google/Anthropic); the Warren-Wyden-Blumenthal letter (Feb. 4, 2026) asking the FTC/DOJ to scrutinize the "acqui-hires" that circumvent antitrust (Meta-Scale AI $14.3B, NVIDIA-Groq ~$20B, Google-Windsurf $2.4B); preemptive opposition to any OpenAI bailout ("privatizing profits and socializing losses"); a proposed tax on AI companies and datacenters.

**The defensive victory that counts: preemption.** On July 1, 2025, the Senate strips the federal moratorium on state AI laws (inserted into Trump's megabill) by a vote of **99 to 1** — an unprecedented cross-party coalition (40 attorneys general, ~260 state legislators, 17 Republican governors). Trump strikes back with the December 11, 2025 EO ("AI Litigation Task Force" at the DOJ, threat to BEAD broadband funds, targeting the Colorado AI Act); Scott Wiener: "we will see them in court." A notable ideological reversal: the left now defends federalism and states' rights — traditionally the right's terrain.

**The internal fracture: Abundance vs. anti-monopoly.** The strategic cleavage running through the Democrats: the "abundance" pole (Ezra Klein, Derek Thompson, March 2025 — build more, deregulate in targeted ways) against the anti-monopoly pole (Warren, Open Markets — "neoliberal repackaging," funded at >$120M including Open Philanthropy). Mamdani's victory in the NYC primary ("abundance for the 99 percent": public housing, buses, childcare) blurs the cleavage. On AI, the two poles diverge on everything except one point: neither defends the status quo.

## 2. The American Union Front: Defensive but Operational

- **Hollywood (2023) remains the matrix**: the WGA and SAG-AFTRA strikes produced the world's first contractual frameworks on "digital replicas" (consent, disclosure, compensation) — with documented loopholes (exemption for high earners, future consent via "reasonably specific description").
- **The SAG-AFTRA video game strike (July 2024 - June 2025, 320 days)**: triggered *over AI*, ratified at 95.04% (July 9, 2025). The 2025 Interactive Media Agreement prohibits capturing future consent at hiring and requires a usage report for digital replicas. The next test: the TV/Theatrical renegotiation (the contract expires in June 2026).
- **Teamsters vs. autonomous trucks**: bills passed overwhelmingly in California (CARS Package) but vetoed twice by Newsom; Tulchin poll: 79% of California voters favor a mandatory human operator; first contract (Local 745, Dallas) requiring a Teamsters operator in every autonomous truck. December 2025: California considers lifting the autonomous truck ban via the DMV — the power struggle continues.
- **AFL-CIO/Microsoft (Dec. 2023)**: the largest union neutrality agreement ever granted by a big tech firm, with AI information-sharing. An ambivalent reading: co-design or co-optation. AFL-CIO poll: 70% of workers fear being replaced.
- **The environmental base**: the Memphis battle (Boxtown, a 90% Black neighborhood) against xAI's unpermitted turbines — NO2 +79% (NASA/ESA satellite data), cancer risk 4× the national average, NAACP/SELC/Earthjustice lawsuit under the Clean Air Act (April 2026). And the national wave: ≥25 datacenters canceled in Virginia in 2025, ~$64-100B of projects blocked or delayed by local opposition (Data Center Watch, medium confidence), 12+ states considering moratoria. This is the most dynamic front — and the most cross-class.

## 3. Europe: An Alternative Narrative, a Subjected Practice

**France.** At the Paris AI summit (February 10-11, 2025, €109B of investments announced by Macron), the left responds in scattered ranks: the counter-summit "For a Humanism of Our Time" by Éric Sadin and the SNJ (~300 participants, an Anti-tech Résistance action that drives Hidalgo from the room); Mélenchon's op-ed accusing Macron of placing France "under the techno-feudal grip of the Elon Musks" (France "acts as a lobbyist to lower the guardrails"); LFI proposes in its program a **network of public datacenters** and a "public domain of data" (referencing the BLOOM/CNRS model); the PS links digital and energy sovereignty (the Ross datacenter bill, passed in the Senate in weakened form, March 2026; Narassiguin: "avoid becoming a mere digital colony"); Green MPs Davi and Belluco carry a **datacenter moratorium** amendment (developed with La Quadrature du Net and "Le nuage était sous nos pieds") — defeated; the simplification law is referred to the Constitutional Council (April 2026). An unusual LFI-PS convergence on the diagnosis ("digital colony"), the classic divergence on the solution (state ownership vs. regulated champions).

**Brussels: defeat in good order.** The 2025-2026 sequence is a series of retreats: withdrawal of the **AI Liability Directive** (February 2025, condemned by the ETUC), then the **digital Omnibus** (proposed November 2025, political agreement May 6-7, 2026) which postpones the AI Act's high-risk obligations to December 2027/August 2028 — "deferral rather than dismantling" (Gibson Dunn). The European left, in the minority, "spent its political capital" (Laura Caroli) to save one gain: the ban on "nudifiers" and generated CSAM. The Greens/EFA (van Sparrentak, Gregorová) denounce a reopening of the GDPR/AI Act "under pressure from the Trump administration and Big Tech lobbies."

**The terrain where the European left wins: labor.** The **Platform Work Directive** (2024, transposition due December 2026) is the only enforceable corpus in the world governing algorithmic management: presumption of employment, right to an explanation of automated decisions, collective bargaining. The ETUC demands its extension to all employees (an "AI at work" directive within the Quality Jobs Act) — Commission survey: 74% want fully automated decisions banned. And the most offensive voice is Spanish: **Yolanda Díaz** (Sumar, Labor Minister) proposes at the EPSCO Council (March 2026) a right to algorithmic transparency and a **tax on AI companies** funding a just-transition fund — AI being meant to *reduce working time*. In Germany, Die Linke frames the question as class struggle ("when datacenters consume energy while welfare offices close, that is not innovation but class struggle from above") and the Greens denounce a "purely investor-driven" expansion with no needs assessment.

**UK: the inverted exception.** Starmer's Labour is *the* pro-AI government in Europe ("mainline AI into the veins," the Clifford plan, January 2025, the £400B figure coming from a Google-commissioned report). Opposition comes from the unions (TUC: a complete AI-at-work bill, ready to legislate since 2024 — WAIRA, right to explanation, ban on emotion recognition) and the creative industries (the copyright battle), not from a structured parliamentary left wing.

## 4. China: Critique Absorbed by the State

The Chinese case inverts the question: social criticism of AI there has *more* legislative effects than in the West, but without autonomous worker power.

- **The founding moment**: "Delivery Riders, Trapped in the System" (Renwu, September 2020) — the viral investigation into the algorithmic compression of delivery times (1h for 3 km in 2016 → 38 min in 2018; one rider dead or injured every 2.5 days in Shanghai in H1 2017). Sun Ping's concept (Academy of Social Sciences): the "inverse algorithm," forced disobedience as a work practice. The timing is political: the article appears at the start of the platform crackdown; the critique becomes tolerated because it serves the re-assertion of control.
- **The "absorb + repress" model**: riders' strikes and self-organization repressed (arrests), while the state legislates on the cause of the anger — the "moderate algorithm" directive (2021), the eight-agency guidelines (May 2025), and the **Central Committee/State Council directive of April 26, 2026**: for ~200M flexible workers (~84M gig workers strictly speaking), local minimum wage, hour caps enforced *by the app itself*, algorithmic transparency subject to collective bargaining (via the official ACFTU union), compliance demanded within ~18 months. 15 of the 16 major platforms signed algorithmic agreements by the end of 2025 (ACFTU figures, to be read as official communication).
- **996.ICU, the boomerang**: the 2019 GitHub repo ("developers' lives matter," 200k stars in 15 days, blocked by Tencent/Alibaba browsers) led the Supreme Court to declare 996 illegal (August 2021). Documented irony: in 2025, the 996 model migrates to Silicon Valley AI startups (Fortune), indirectly endorsed by Sergey Brin ("60h sweet spot"). Chinese social criticism now applies to San Francisco.
- **The intellectual new left**: Wang Hui (Tsinghua) does not write directly about AI — his framework (the critique of "depoliticization," of neoliberalism) is applied by others ("postsocialist AI," Qiaoyu Cai, 2025). The most rigorous sociological critique of Chinese platform labor is carried from Hong Kong (Jenny Chan, Pun Ngai — *Dying for an iPhone*). Xi's "common prosperity" (2021, ~$1T of market capitalization erased) disciplined the platforms without real redistribution ("surprisingly meager," China Leadership Monitor): a facade Maoism serving the recentering of power.

## 5. Japan, Korea, India: Three Models of Resistance

- **Japan — the creators' resistance.** The most active anti-AI front is not union-based but artistic: 26 major voice actors (Yamadera, Park, Nakao) form "No More Mudan Seisei AI" (October 2024) against unconsented voice cloning; an institutional victory in April 2026, with a Justice Ministry panel classifying "voice rights" alongside personality rights. The GPT-4o "Ghibli-style" wave (March 2025, >700M images) reactivated Miyazaki's "insult to life itself" — Ghibli remaining officially silent. The Japanese Communist Party demands a risk-based framework law (the EU model) against the government's "promotion only" AI Promotion Act (cited poll: 59.7% want stronger regulation).
- **Korea — institutionalization.** The region's most powerful model: AI Basic Act (January 2025, in force 2026), the **Yellow Envelope Act** (passed 183-3, in force March 2026) which opens unionization to non-employees (gig workers) and broadens the definition of employer to the entity that "practically dominates" the worker — ~100,000 bargaining requests in 2 days. The KCTU (>1M members) has sat since February 2026 on a monthly state consultative body on AI.
- **India — autonomy without leverage.** The IFAT (45,000 drivers/riders, leader Shaik Salauddin) carries the most *data-centered* struggle: algorithmic transparency, distrust of Aadhaar (workers are actively discouraged from linking their biometric identity to the platforms). Documented (Dec. 2025): in Bengaluru, platforms use GPS dashboards to spot rider gatherings and disperse them by assigning rides in opposite directions — the algorithm as a literal union-busting tool. Karnataka's gig law (2025) requires algorithm explanations but without collective rights: symbolic.

## 6. Synthesis: The Left's Five Real Levers

The answer to the initial question ("outpaced?") is: **no, but fragmented across five levers of very unequal effectiveness.**

| Lever | Where it works | Limit |
|---|---|---|
| **1. The collective contract** | SAG-AFTRA (digital replicas), Teamsters (human operator), EU platform directive, Korean Yellow Envelope Act | Defensive, sectoral, always one model behind |
| **2. Algorithmic labor law** | EU 2024 directive (presumption of employment, right to explanation) — the only enforceable corpus in the world | Slow transposition, "all employees" extension blocked |
| **3. The terrain (water, air, land)** | Memphis/NAACP, Virginia (25 cancellations), moratoria (Ireland 2021-25, Netherlands), FR amendments | Local victories vs. $400B/year capex; Ireland eventually reopened |
| **4. Antitrust and taxation** | Warren (acqui-hires), Díaz tax, Sanders robot tax, PS datacenter bill | Proposals without majorities; the EU retreats (Omnibus) |
| **5. The public alternative** | Public datacenters (LFI), EuroStack (Bria/Mazzucato), public domain of data | "An intellectual horizon without means at scale"; private champions vs. commons ambiguity |

**Three cross-cutting observations:**
1. **The left wins where it already has institutions** (US entertainment unions, Nordic and Korean social dialogue, European labor law) and loses where they would have to be built (compute, capital, infrastructure). It regulates AI's *uses*, never its *production*.
2. **The cleavage is no longer pro/anti-tech but preemption/democracy**: on both sides of the Atlantic, the decisive battle of 2025-2026 was to prevent the central power (Trump, the Commission) from disarming the lower levels (US states, national law, local authorities). The left won its biggest victory there (99-1 in the US Senate) — in coalition with part of the right.
3. **The Chinese paradox illuminates the rest**: where the state wants to discipline the platforms, worker protections advance faster than elsewhere — without union freedom. The question posed to the Western left: are its defensive victories (consent, explanation, moratoria) worth more than an effective authoritarian regulation? Part 15 (two concentrations of power) finds its social translation here.
